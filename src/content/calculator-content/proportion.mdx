A proportion is a statement that two ratios are equal, written as `a / b = c / d`.
This proportion calculator takes any three of the four terms and solves for the
missing one using cross-multiplication, so you can check ratio problems, scale a
recipe, or convert a rate without doing the algebra by hand.

## How to use this calculator

1. Enter the first ratio's two terms, **a** and **b**.
2. Enter the known term of the second ratio, **c**.
3. The calculator solves for the missing fourth term, **d**, so that `a / b = c / d`.

## Worked example

Take the default inputs: `a = 2`, `b = 3`, `c = 4`.

```
a / b = c / d
2 / 3 = 4 / d
```

Cross-multiply and solve for `d`:

```
d = (b × c) / a
d = (3 × 4) / 2
d = 12 / 2
d = 6
```

The completed proportion is `2 / 3 = 4 / 6`. Both ratios simplify the same way —
2/3 and 4/6 both reduce to about 0.6667 — which confirms the answer is correct.
For the full method behind solving for an unknown term this way, see
[OpenStax's guide to solving proportions and their applications](https://openstax.org/books/prealgebra-2e/pages/6-5-solve-proportions-and-their-applications).

## Frequently asked questions

### How do I calculate a proportion by hand?

To calculate a proportion, cross-multiply the two ratios (multiply the numerator
of one side by the denominator of the other) and divide to isolate the unknown
term. This calculator does that same cross-multiplication step automatically.

### What is ratio and proportion, and how are they different?

A ratio compares two numbers, like `2 to 3`. A proportion is an equation stating
that two ratios are equal, like `2 / 3 = 4 / 6`. Ratio and proportion problems
usually give you three known numbers and ask you to solve for the fourth.

### Is this the same as a sample proportion in statistics?

No. This tool solves numeric ratio equations (`a / b = c / d`), not statistical
hypothesis tests. A sample proportion in statistics refers to the share of a
sample with a given trait — for example, the fraction of survey respondents who
answered "yes" — usually expressed as a fraction or percentage rather than a
missing term in a ratio equation.
